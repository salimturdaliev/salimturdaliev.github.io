Research brief / Behavioral intervention

Nudging Energy Conservation in Armenia

Why social-comparison reports continued to save electricity after a long pause and recovered their strength when restarted.

Setting: ArmeniaDesign: Randomized controlled trialIntervention: Energy reports

Would you use less electricity if you knew your neighbors were saving more than you?

That question sits at the heart of a randomized controlled trial conducted in Armenia in 2022 and 2023. Households received regular, easy-to-read reports comparing their electricity use with that of similar households. Social comparison is familiar in behavioral economics, but much less is known about whether it keeps working over time in settings outside Western Europe and the United States.

The experiment

Households were randomly assigned to receive different versions of the reports. One group received social-comparison feedback. Another received the same feedback plus information about costs. A control group received no reports. Electricity consumption was observed over a sufficiently long period to examine persistence rather than only short-run novelty effects.

Households receiving the reports used less electricity than those in the control group. The more unusual part of the experiment came next: the intervention was not continuous.

What happened when the reports stopped?

The reports were paused for eight months. If nudges work mainly by briefly attracting attention, a pause this long should eliminate the effect. If they create learning or new routines, some conservation might remain.

During the pause, the savings did not vanish. Roughly half of the original effect persisted even though households received no new letters. This suggests that the intervention produced something more durable than a one-month attention shock. Households may have learned what normal electricity use looks like, adopted new routines, or continued monitoring their consumption.

Key timing result

A meaningful share of electricity savings survived an eight-month interruption in the intervention.

Could the nudge be reactivated?

After the pause, the reports were reintroduced. A reasonable concern was that recipients might be fatigued or desensitized because the message was no longer new. Instead, the conservation effect returned at approximately the same strength as during the initial treatment phase.

Together, persistence during the pause and recovery after the restart suggest a different way to think about behavioral policy. Social-comparison reports need not be framed as messages that must be sent every month forever. They can be treated as a policy instrument with a delivery schedule.

Why intermittent delivery may work

Social-comparison feedback does more than remind. Electricity use is difficult to perceive in daily life, and households often lack a clear benchmark. Reports help people calibrate their behavior. That benchmark can remain useful even when the signal is temporarily absent.

Changed routines around heating, lighting, appliance use, or monitoring may also become partly self-sustaining. Finally, a pause may reduce message fatigue and reset attention, helping explain why the restarted intervention recovered its original impact.

Lessons for policymakers

Behavioral interventions do not replace prices, infrastructure, or regulation. They can, however, complement traditional policies when budgets and administrative capacity are limited or when reforms that raise household bills are politically difficult.

The timing results make intermittent delivery a realistic design option. Policymakers can ask how long an intervention can pause before benefits decay too much, which cycle is most cost-effective, and how delivery can be scheduled to reduce both cost and fatigue.

This evidence does not imply that reports can stop forever or be reactivated indefinitely without adaptation. It supports a more practical proposition: well-designed cycles can preserve substantial savings while making behavioral programs easier to operate.
