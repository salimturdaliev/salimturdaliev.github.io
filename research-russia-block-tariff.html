Research brief / Energy pricing

Russia's Block Tariff Experiment

Energy savings, dirty fuels, and the unintended consequences of nonlinear electricity pricing.

Setting: RussiaDesign: Natural experimentPolicy: Increasing block tariffs

In 2013, Russia launched a bold experiment. Several regions introduced a new electricity pricing system: the more a household consumed, the higher the price for additional units.

Known as increasing block tariffs, the reform was an attempt to move toward market-based energy pricing and away from decades of heavy subsidies. At first glance, it looked promising. A country known for high energy use might finally curb demand and move toward greater efficiency. The reality was more complicated.

The promise and the reality

Russian households proved far less responsive to electricity price changes than expected. Long-run demand elasticity was around -0.1. Average consumption fell by only about 0.56% per year, corresponding to annual carbon reductions of approximately 0.3 million tonnes.

These effects were substantially smaller than policymakers had hoped for. The price signal alone was not enough to produce a large change in household electricity demand.

A surprising turn

Households did not respond only by reducing consumption. Many purchased more major appliances, including refrigerators, washing machines, and air conditioners. The tariff may have increased the value of investing in newer and potentially more efficient durables.

Among poorer families, however, another response emerged. Facing higher electricity bills, some households switched toward cheaper but dirtier fuels such as coal, firewood, and kerosene. These alternatives create more pollution and increase indoor health risks.

Key finding

In the regions affected by increasing block tariffs, the probability of purchasing dirty fuels rose substantially, with the largest response among vulnerable households.

Lessons for policymakers

Russia's experiment illustrates why the distributional effects of energy-price reform matter. Price-based tools can encourage efficiency, but without strong social protection they may deepen inequality and generate environmental spillovers in other fuels.

Pricing reforms should therefore be accompanied by targeted support, affordable clean-fuel alternatives, and safety nets that protect vulnerable households. A policy designed to reduce electricity demand should be evaluated across the household's complete energy portfolio, not electricity consumption alone.
