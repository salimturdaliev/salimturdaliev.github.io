Research brief / Energy insecurity

Electricity Outages and Child Growth in Kyrgyzstan

How unreliable electricity can affect children's long-term health even where nearly every household is connected to the grid.

Setting: Kyrgyz RepublicData: Household panelOutcome: Child growth

Electricity is more than light in a home. It keeps food fresh, powers heating during freezing winters, and supports access to clean water.

In the Kyrgyz Republic, almost every household is connected to the electricity grid. Yet connection alone does not guarantee access to reliable power. Frequent blackouts force families to fall back on fuels such as wood, coal, or dung for heating and cooking. These alternatives help households cope, but they also worsen indoor air quality and living conditions.

The study

Using household panel data from across Kyrgyzstan, the research examined how frequent electricity outages influence the growth of children under five. Growth was measured using height-for-age scores, a widely used indicator of childhood stunting and long-term well-being.

The findings

0.37 SD

Children in households experiencing outages more than once a month had height-for-age scores about 0.37 standard deviations lower than otherwise similar children with more reliable electricity.

Larger effect for girls

The negative association was markedly stronger for girls, raising concerns about gender differences in how scarce household resources are allocated.

Long-term measure

Weight-for-age was not significantly affected, while the height result points to the cumulative effects of repeated energy disruption.

Why it matters

Electricity reliability is often discussed as an issue of comfort, firm productivity, or macroeconomic development. These results show that it can also shape children's health and future potential.

In a country with harsh winters and limited household resources, energy insecurity may translate quietly into stunted growth and poorer life chances. The stronger result for girls also places electricity reliability within a broader discussion of gender equality.

Lessons for policymakers

Expanding grid connections is only the first step. Reliable supply matters just as much. Infrastructure investment, better pricing strategies, and targeted social protection can reduce outages and limit the need for households to rely on harmful alternatives.

Energy policy should therefore be evaluated partly as health and human-development policy. Improving reliability can support child health, reduce exposure to indoor pollution, and protect families from coping strategies with long-term costs.

Reference

  • Sargsyan, Y., & Turdaliev, S. (2023). Powering up child growth: The impact of electricity outages on children's anthropometric outcomes in Kyrgyz Republic. Eastern European Economics, 62(4). Article
