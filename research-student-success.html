Research brief / Economics of education

What Drives Success in First-Year Statistics?

Evidence on engagement, online learning, prior preparation, and the constraints students face outside the classroom.

Setting: University statisticsData: Course recordsApproach: Multivariate analysis

Introductory statistics is a gateway course for many degree programs. Understanding why some students thrive while others struggle can inform course design and academic support.

Data and approach

The study combines administrative and course records from a first-year statistics class. Student outcomes are linked to attendance, use of online learning materials, prior preparation, employment and family commitments, and demographic characteristics using multivariate models.

The findings are associative rather than uniformly causal. They nevertheless identify clear priorities for instructors and universities.

Positive associations

  • Regular engagement. Class attendance and active use of online materials, including downloads and practice resources, were the strongest and most consistent positive predictors of grades.
  • Groups that performed well. In this sample, female students and students from outside the capital tended to outperform peers after accounting for other observed characteristics.

Barriers to performance

  • Time constraints. Employment during the term and family obligations reduce available study time and were associated with lower performance.
  • Low perceived importance. Students who did not view the course as important engaged less and earned lower marks.
  • Preparation gaps. Differences related to prior schooling, including language of instruction, pointed to uneven preparation in foundational mathematics and statistics.

A measure intended to capture steady week-to-week effort was informative but imperfect, illustrating the difficulty of reducing consistent engagement to a single indicator.

Why it matters

The most reliable levers are attendance, structured weekly engagement, and accessible opportunities to practice. Time scarcity and preparation gaps can undermine performance even among motivated students.

Practical message

Universities can improve support without redesigning the entire curriculum: diagnose gaps early, make weekly practice predictable, provide rapid feedback, and design resources for students with limited time.

Implications for universities and instructors

  • Reward regular contact. Use brief retrieval checks or participation credit to make attendance valuable.
  • Treat the learning platform as a second classroom. Provide weekly checklists, low-stakes practice, rapid feedback, and clear progress signals.
  • Bridge gaps early. Use first-week diagnostics and just-in-time refreshers in algebra, probability, and basic statistics.
  • Design for scarce time. Offer short practice blocks, predictable deadlines, and assessments that are not unnecessarily concentrated.
  • Support constrained students. Provide flexible office hours, study groups, and referral pathways for students balancing work or family responsibilities.
