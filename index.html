About

Evidence for better energy and social policy.

Using experimental, quasi-experimental, and econometric methods, my research explores how energy reforms and policies shape household behavior, welfare, and environmental outcomes. My methods include randomized controlled trials, natural experiments, difference-in-differences, matching, causal inference, Markov modeling, and cost-effectiveness and welfare analysis.

My work focuses on electricity pricing reforms, subsidies, behavioral interventions such as peer comparison reports, and spillovers across electricity and gas. I also study energy insecurity, including how outages affect children's health and how pricing reforms can unintentionally push households toward dirtier fuels.

Beyond academic research, I have contributed to policy evaluation for the European Commission, Eurofound, DG HOME, and other international organizations. I have taught econometrics, statistics, policy evaluation, and energy and environmental economics at universities in Prague and Tashkent.

01 Energy pricingRussia's block tariff experiment Read

Energy savings, dirty fuels, and unintended consequences.

In 2013, several Russian regions introduced increasing block tariffs: households paid a higher price for additional electricity once consumption crossed a threshold. The reform aimed to move toward market-based energy pricing and away from heavy subsidies.

The promise and the reality

Households proved far less responsive to price changes than expected. Long-run electricity demand elasticity was around -0.1, and average consumption fell by only about 0.56% per year. The estimated carbon reduction was modest relative to the scale of Russia's emissions.

How households adapted

Some households purchased more major appliances, partly because the tariff increased the value of efficiency investments. Among poorer families, however, higher bills encouraged switching toward coal, firewood, and kerosene. In the affected regions, the probability of purchasing dirty fuels increased substantially, especially among vulnerable households.

Policy lesson

Price-based reforms can encourage efficiency, but they can also deepen inequality and produce environmental spillovers. Pricing signals should be combined with targeted support, affordable clean-fuel alternatives, and social protection for vulnerable households.

02 Behavioral interventionNudging energy conservation in Armenia Read

Why intermittent social-comparison reports continued to work.

In 2022 and 2023, we ran a randomized controlled trial in Armenia. Households received easy-to-read reports comparing their electricity use with similar households. Some reports also included cost information, while a control group received no reports.

Persistence through a pause

Households receiving reports used less electricity. The intervention was then paused for eight months. Savings did not disappear: roughly half of the initial effect persisted even though no new letters arrived, suggesting learning, calibration, or habit-like changes rather than a short-lived attention effect.

Reactivation without fatigue

When reports restarted, the conservation effect returned at approximately its original strength. This result suggests that behavioral interventions can be designed in deliberate cycles instead of being delivered every month indefinitely.

Policy lesson

Timing is itself a policy design choice. Intermittent delivery may lower administrative costs and reduce message fatigue while preserving meaningful energy savings. Behavioral tools do not replace prices or infrastructure, but they can complement them without increasing household bills.

03 Energy insecurityElectricity outages and child growth in Kyrgyzstan Read

Reliable electricity matters for health, not only economic productivity.

Using household panel data from the Kyrgyz Republic, we examined how frequent electricity outages affect children under five. Height-for-age scores provide a measure of childhood stunting and long-term well-being.

Findings

  • Children in households facing outages more than once a month had height-for-age scores about 0.37 standard deviations lower than comparable children with more reliable electricity.
  • The negative association was substantially stronger for girls, suggesting gender differences in how scarce household resources are allocated.
  • Weight-for-age was not significantly affected, while the height results point to lasting consequences of repeated disruption.

Policy lesson

Grid access is not enough. Reliability, infrastructure investment, pricing strategy, and targeted social protection all matter for child health, gender equality, and long-term development.

04 Economics of educationWhat drives success in first-year statistics? Read

Evidence on engagement, preparation, and constraints facing students.

We linked outcomes in a first-year statistics course to attendance, use of online learning materials, prior preparation, and student characteristics. The results are associative rather than uniformly causal, but they identify practical priorities for teaching and student support.

Key findings

  • Regular attendance and active use of online materials were the strongest and most consistent positive predictors of grades.
  • Employment during term, family obligations, and low perceived course importance were associated with lower performance.
  • Prior schooling and language-of-instruction differences indicated preparation gaps in foundational mathematics and statistics.

Implications

Universities can support students through early diagnostics, targeted refreshers, predictable weekly practice, rapid feedback, modular resources, and flexible support for students balancing study with work or family responsibilities.
